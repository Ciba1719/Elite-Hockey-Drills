01 — The Method

Train for the long game.

You don't have less to gain from training after 40 — you have different things to gain. The research on the aging athlete is clear: tendon stiffness, joint quality, mitochondrial function, and rate of force development all decline with age — and every one of them is still highly trainable when you train them the right way. Most programs written for "fitness over 40" miss this. They either treat you like you're fragile (you're not) or ignore what your body actually does best now: recover slower, but adapt deeper. The priority order through these five days is backed by the literature: tissue quality first, then mobility, then power, then strength, then conditioning.

01

Tissue First

Tendons and joints take longer to adapt than muscle. We train them deliberately — slow eccentrics, sustained isometrics, heavy-slow-resistance principles drawn straight from elite rehab and prehab protocols.

02

Power, Maintained

RFD declines with age, but it doesn't have to crash. Low-volume, high-quality plyometric and ballistic work — done well, done often — keeps the first step explosive and the shot heavy.

03

Zone 2 over HIIT

A bigger aerobic base means faster between-shift recovery and a heart that doesn't have to work as hard. We build it the way the mitochondrial science says to — slow, steady, often.

"After 40, the goal isn't to train harder than you did at 25 — it's to train smarter than you ever have."
02 — How To Use This

Five days. One week.

Pick five days that work for you — they don't have to be Monday through Friday. Place Day 3 (Zone 2) on a day you have more time. Leave at least one full rest day in the middle. Each session runs 30 to 45 minutes including warm-up and cool-down. You need a small open space and a floor. A loaded backpack helps on Day 2. No other equipment. If something doesn't feel right — sharp pain, joint pain, anything that lingers — stop. You've earned the right to be smart about this. Modify or skip and come back stronger.

How To Read a Workout

Every exercise card has the same parts. Tap any exercise name — in blue — to open a demo video and the full step-by-step guide on the site.

Sets × Reps
The volume — how many sets, and how many reps or seconds each.
Tempo
The speed of each rep — and on this program, often the whole point. A 4-2-1-0 tempo means 4 seconds lowering, 2 seconds pause at the bottom, 1 second up, 0 rest at the top. The slow eccentric is what builds tendon resilience.
Rest · Cue · Builds
How long to wait before the next set, the one thing to focus on, and the hockey reason for the work.
Before Every Session

The warm-up.

Five minutes. Every session. Non-negotiable — especially now. The older the athlete, the more they need to wake the tissues up before asking them to perform. This sequence covers everything that matters for hockey: hips, T-spine, ankles, shoulders. Move with control. Breathe through your nose where you can.

· Warm-Up~5 min
0190/90 Hip Switch↗
2 rounds6 each side · slow tempo

Sit on the floor. Front leg bent at 90° in front of you, back leg bent 90° to the side. Lean forward gently over the front shin and feel the hip open. Then — keeping both knees on the floor — rotate the hips to switch sides. Both knees should land at 90° in the new position.

"Move slowly. Let the hips open."

Builds — Hip internal and external rotation — the joint range skating quietly takes from you.

02World's Greatest Stretch↗
2 rounds3 each side · controlled

From a push-up position, step your right foot up beside your right hand. Drop your right elbow toward the inside of your right foot. Then rotate and reach the same arm up to the ceiling, following with your eyes. Place the hand back down, step back, switch sides.

"Open chest. Open hips. Long spine."

Builds — Full-body mobility prep — hips, T-spine, and shoulders all in one movement.

03Cat-Cow + Thread the Needle↗
2 rounds8 cat-cow + 3 each side · slow

On hands and knees. Cat-cow: arch the back up toward the ceiling (cat), then drop the belly and lift the chest (cow). Eight slow reps. Thread the needle: reach your right arm under and across your body, drop the shoulder to the floor, hold 3 seconds. Switch.

"Slow. Long exhale on every move."

Builds — Spine articulation and mid-back mobility — where most of the stiffness lives after 40.

Day 1
Move Better First

Movement Quality & Mobility

~40 minBodyweight only

Day 1 is the most important day of the week. Everything else is built on top of it. Today is about restoring the joint range and movement patterns that life — sitting, driving, the same skating pattern over and over — has quietly taken from you. No load. No intensity. Pure quality. Begin with the warm-up — always.

· The Workout5 movements
01Cossack Squat↗
3 × 6each side · Tempo 3-1-1-0REST 60s

Stand wide — feet about twice shoulder-width, toes slightly out. Shift weight onto your right leg and bend that knee deep. Left leg stays straight, heel down, toes up. Push back to center and switch sides. Hold a small counterweight if balance is tough.

"Heel down on the straight leg. Don't rush the switch."

Builds — Hip and adductor mobility under load — directly relevant to skating stance and crossover depth.

02Half-Kneeling T-Spine Rotation↗
3 × 8each side · slowREST 45s

Kneel on one knee, the other foot flat in front (both knees at 90°). Place hands behind your head with elbows wide. Rotate your upper body slowly toward the front leg, then back. Hips stay perfectly still — only the spine moves.

"Rotate from the ribs. Hips locked."

Builds — Thoracic rotation — the range you need for a clean shoulder check and a powerful shot.

03Bird Dog with 3-Second Hold↗
3 × 6each sideREST 45s

On hands and knees. Slowly extend your right arm forward and left leg back — perfectly horizontal, head to heel. Hold three seconds. Hips don't tip or twist — imagine a glass of water on your lower back. Switch sides.

"Long line, head to heel. Don't tip the hips."

Builds — Anti-rotation core control — the spinal stability that protects your back during every twist on the ice.

04Slow Reverse Lunge↗
3 × 6each leg · Tempo 3-1-1-0REST 60s

Stand tall. Step one foot back and slowly lower the back knee toward the floor over three seconds. Stop just before the knee touches. Drive through the front heel to stand up. The slow descent is the whole point.

"Slow on the way down. Smooth on the way up."

Builds — Single-leg strength, knee control, balance — three things that quietly fade after 40 if you don't train them.

05Wall Slide + Reach↗
3 × 10slowREST 45s

Back, head, and arms pressed against a wall. Elbows bent at 90°, backs of hands touching the wall at ear level. Slowly slide your arms up overhead, keeping every point of contact the whole way. Slide back down.

"Wrists pinned to the wall. Don't fake it with the lower back."

Builds — Shoulder mobility and upper-back posture — the antidote to a desk job and a hockey stance.

Cool-Down — 3 min

Knees to chest, rock side to side 60s. Box breathing — in 4, hold 4, out 6 — for two minutes. Long exhales win.

Day 2
Build the Frame

Tendon Resilience & Strength

~40 minBackpack (5–15 kg)

Tendons adapt slower than muscle. They respond best to slow loading — long time under tension, controlled eccentrics, sustained isometrics. This is the heavy-slow-resistance principle, drawn straight from the orthopedic and sport-science literature. Today the tempos matter more than the reps. Honor them. You'll need a loaded backpack (books, water bottles — 5–15 kg). Begin with the warm-up.

Tap any exercise to watch the demo and open the full guide
· The Workout5 movements
01Tempo Goblet Squat (Backpack)↗
3 × 8Tempo 4-2-1-0REST 90s

Hold a loaded backpack against your chest. Feet shoulder-width. Squat down over four full seconds. Pause two seconds at the bottom. Drive up in one. No rest at the top — straight into the next rep.

"Don't cheat the eccentric. The slow descent is the work."

Builds — Quad and patellar tendon adaptation via slow controlled loading — the protocol that keeps knees pain-free long-term.

02Spanish Squat — Isometric Hold↗
3 × 30sholdREST 60s

Loop a belt or strap around a sturdy pole at knee height, then around the back of your knees. Lean back until it's taut, squat to a 60° knee bend, and hold. The strap takes shear off the knee and loads the quad and patellar tendon directly.

"Sit back into the strap. Knees don't slide forward."

Builds — Patellar tendon resilience — the most-researched isometric protocol for chronic knee pain in the literature.

03Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift↗
3 × 8each side · Tempo 3-1-1-0REST 60s

Stand on one leg, slight bend in the knee. Hinge at the hip — lower your chest as the back leg extends behind you. Back leg and torso form one long line. Lower until you feel a hamstring stretch, then return.

"Long line — head, hips, heel."

Builds — Hamstring tendon resilience and posterior chain strength. Critical for protecting the lower back and powering the skating stride.

04Tempo Push-Up↗
3 × 6Tempo 4-1-2-0REST 60s

Push-up position (knees down is fine — quality over ego). Lower over four seconds. Pause one inch off the floor for one second. Press up over two. Body in a straight line — no sagging hips.

"Squeeze the floor away on the way up."

Builds — Shoulder and elbow tendon resilience. Slow eccentrics protect joints that take a beating every season.

05Tibialis Raise↗
2 × 15Tempo 2-1-2-0REST 45s

Stand with heels 15 cm from a wall, lean lightly back against it. Heels stay on the floor. Lift your toes up toward your shins as high as possible. Lower slowly. Burn down the front of the shin.

"Toes high. Slow on the way back down."

Builds — Anterior tibialis strength — prevents shin splints, improves balance, protects the ankle in cuts.

Cool-Down — 3 min

Lying hamstring stretch, 60s each side. Box breathing — in 4, hold 4, out 4, hold 4 — four cycles.

Day 3
The Engine

Aerobic Base · Zone 2

~35–45 minContinuous low intensity

This is the day that does the most for your hockey, your recovery, and your life — and the day almost every adult athlete underdoes. Zone 2 is conversational-pace cardio: slow enough that you can talk in full sentences, fast enough that you'd rather not. Low-intensity, long-duration work builds mitochondrial density — the cellular machinery that produces energy and clears fatigue. It declines with age, but unlike most age-related declines, this one reverses with the right stimulus. A bigger aerobic base means faster between-shift recovery, a lower resting heart rate, and a body that's harder to fatigue.

·

Duration

30–45 minutes

Continuous. Same intensity start to finish. The first 10 minutes will feel almost too easy — that's correct. Stay there.

·

Intensity

180 minus your age

Heart-rate ceiling. A 45-year-old caps at 135 bpm. No monitor? Use the talk test: you should hold a full conversation with mild effort.

·

Mode — pick one

Walk · Bike · Jog · Swim · Row

Brisk walk on an incline. Easy bike (stationary or road). Easy jog. Swim. Rowing machine. Pick what your joints prefer.

·

The Rule

If you can't talk, too hard

If you can sing, too easy. The benefit is in the volume of low-intensity work, not the intensity itself. The opposite of every HIIT video — and exactly what your body responds to best now.

"The first ten minutes will feel almost too easy. That's correct. Stay there. The benefit is in the volume."

Day 4
Keep It Fast

Power Maintenance

~30 minHigh quality · low volume

Power — the ability to produce force quickly — is the most age-sensitive quality in sport. Rate of Force Development declines faster than maximum strength. But it doesn't have to crash. The fix isn't more volume. It's high-quality power work, done often, with full recovery between reps. Every rep should look identical to the first. The rule today: full rest between sets. Stop the moment quality drops. This is the opposite of training to fatigue. Begin with the warm-up.

· The Workout5 movements
01Low Pogo Hops↗
3 × 8quick contactsREST 90s

Stand tall. Bounce on the balls of your feet, legs nearly straight. The bounce comes from your ankles and calves, not your knees. Low amplitude. Quick, light, quiet floor contacts. Like skipping rope without the rope.

"Quick feet. Floor is hot."

Builds — Ankle stiffness and reactive strength — the elastic quality that gives you a first step.

02Box Step-Up Drive↗
3 × 4each leg · full restREST 90s

A sturdy bench or step at about knee height. Place one foot on the step. Drive up explosively, bringing the opposite knee up to hip height as you stand. Land softly back down. Reset fully. The drive is the point — not the height.

"Punch the knee up. Fast."

Builds — Concentric leg power — the exact pattern of pushing off the back leg during a stride.

03Lateral Bound — Stick It↗
3 × 3each side · full restREST 90s

Stand on one leg. Push off sideways and land on the other leg only. Hold that landing for two full seconds — perfectly balanced, knee bent, no wobble. The hold is non-negotiable. Then push back. That's two reps.

"Land. Freeze. Reset."

Builds — The exact biomechanics of a skating push-off — plus the eccentric control to absorb the next stride.

04Rotational Throw↗
3 × 5each side · med ball or loaded bagREST 90s

Stand sideways to a sturdy wall, about 2 m away. Hold a medicine ball (or a small loaded backpack with a handle) at hip height. Rotate hips and shoulders quickly, throwing the ball into the wall. Catch on the bounce. Reset between reps — there's no rush.

"Hips lead. Arms follow."

Builds — Rotational power — the engine behind a hard shot and a quick body check.

05Plyo Push-Up to Pause↗
3 × 4full rest · hands elevated OKREST 90s

Push-up position — hands on a sturdy bench or low chair if needed. Lower fast. Push up explosively so the hands leave the surface briefly. Land softly. Pause two full seconds. Then the next rep. Full effort every single time.

"Push the floor away — hard."

Builds — Upper-body power — for stick battles, board work, and explosive pushing.

Cool-Down — 3 min

Walk 90 seconds. Calf stretch, 30s each side. Couch stretch (hip flexor), 30s each side. Done.

Day 5
Tie It Together

Integration & Recovery

~40 minTwo blocks

Day 5 is the integration session. You'll move through hockey-specific patterns in a circuit that mimics the work-to-rest demands of the game. Then you'll spend the back half on something most adult athletes skip entirely: deliberate recovery. Both halves matter. Block A teaches your body to apply what you built this week. Block B is what lets you do it all again next week. Begin with the warm-up.

B Deliberate Recovery~6 min · don't skip

This is what lets you train again next week. Slow it all down and let the nervous system downshift.

B1Couch Stretch↗
90s each side

Back foot up on a couch or chair, front leg in a lunge. Squeeze the back glute hard. The stretch hits the hip flexor and quad.

B290/90 Breathing↗
3 min

Lie on your back, feet flat on a chair, knees and hips at 90°. Hands on your ribs. In slowly through the nose. Long exhale through the mouth. Re-set the diaphragm and downshift the nervous system.

The Other Half

Recovery is where the adaptation happens.

At 25, you could outwork bad recovery. At 40+, you can't — and you shouldn't want to. Recovery isn't a soft topic; it's the literal mechanism by which your body becomes stronger. Skip this page and the five training days give you a fraction of what they could. Get this right and everything else compounds.

7–9 hrs

Sleep

Deep sleep is when growth hormone is released and tissue repair happens. After 40, the body produces less of it and you have to work harder to defend the sleep window. Cool room. Dark room. Screens off 60 minutes before bed. Consistent bedtime. This is the single highest-ROI thing on this page.

30 g / meal

Fuel

A palm-sized portion of protein at every main meal — the research on protein needs for the 40+ athlete has shifted upward in the last decade. Fill the rest of the plate with vegetables and fruit (variety matters). Limit ultra-processed foods. No supplement will outperform real food in adequate quantities.

2.5–3.5 L

Hydration

The thirst signal weakens with age — meaning you're often more dehydrated than you feel. Start the day with a glass of water before coffee. Keep a bottle visible. Pale-yellow urine is the target. Even mild dehydration drops power output by 5–10%. Worth fixing.

"You can't out-train bad sleep, bad food, and bad hydration. Especially now."
