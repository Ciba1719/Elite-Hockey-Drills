Read This First
BUILT FOR young players.
This is an off-ice program for hockey players roughly 9 to 11 years old. It is not a small version of an adult workout — it is built from the ground up for growing bodies, with one goal: to make training fun while quietly building the movement, balance, and speed that every great skater is made of.
At this age the best thing we can train is how a child moves. So the program is full of skipping, hopping, balancing, bounding, sprinting, and games — not heavy lifting. There is no barbell, no grinding, and nothing is ever done “to failure.” Just bands, bodyweight, a little space, and a lot of quality reps.
Every session uses the same simple shape and takes about 35–50 minutes (a little longer through the peak weeks). Three sessions a week, eight weeks, two four-week blocks. Block 1 teaches the body to move well and land softly. Block 2 turns that good movement into spring and speed, with plenty of competition and play.
A parent or non-specialist coach can run all of it. Every exercise has a plain-language cue and one “what good looks like” point, plus a video on the site. Your job is simple: keep it fun, keep it quality, and watch a confident athlete grow.
What to expect
Eight weeks will not — and should not — turn a child into a powerhouse. What it builds is better: softer landings, steadier balance, smoother sprinting, and a love of moving well. Those are the foundations that pay off for years on the ice. Train it as written, keep it joyful, and the skill-check pages will show the progress.
TWO RULES · NON-NEGOTIABLE
01 Every landing is soft and “stuck.” We teach the body to absorb and balance before we ask it to bounce — this is how we keep young joints safe.
02 It stays fun. Quality reps, long rest, lots of games. If it ever feels like a grind, make it lighter and more playful — the fun is what builds the athlete.
01 The Method
WHY THIS works.
This is not a random circuit. It follows long-term athletic development — the right things, in the right order, for a 9–11-year-old body.
Move well first
Before speed or strength comes skill: squatting, hinging, balancing, skipping, and landing with control. This is the golden age for learning movement, so we spend it building a clean, athletic toolkit the child will use for life.
Land like a cat
Soft, quiet, balanced landings are the single most important — and most protective — skill at this age. Every jump starts as a stick-and-freeze, so the body learns to absorb and control force long before it learns to rebound.
Coordination & speed mechanics
Young nervous systems learn fast. We groove great sprint mechanics — tall posture, knee up, quick feet — and reactive agility through games, building the coordination that makes a smooth, fast skater.
Spring, not grind
Power comes from low, snappy jumps and light, ballistic throws — never from heavy loads or fatigue. We build springs with quality reps and long rest, so it is safe, sharp, and never a slog.
Single-leg & groin care
Skating happens one leg at a time, so balance, single-leg control, and gentle groin work run through the whole program. Strong, mobile hips early means a more durable skater later.
Fun is the engine
Games, competition, and play are not the reward at the end — they are the method. A child who loves training will train for years, and that consistency beats any single hard session.

02 The Roadmap
THE eight weeks.
Two blocks of four weeks. The work builds for three weeks, then eases off — so the body soaks it up and the child stays fresh and keen.

03 How To Use This
EVERY session.
All 24 sessions share the same simple shape. Run them in order. Total time: about 35–50 minutes — a little longer through the peak weeks.
1 RAMP Warm-Up 8–10 min
Raise, Activate, Mobilize, Potentiate — the same fun warm-up on the next page, done before every session.
2 Movement Primer 6–8 min
A few low hops and soft landings to wake the body up and groove good movement. Always crisp, never tiring.
3 Speed & Skill 12–16 min
The fun, fast skill of the day — done fresh. Full effort on each short rep, with long rest. Quality over quantity.
4 Strength & Control 12–16 min
Bodyweight and band work, balance, and core — built one leg at a time, with control. No grinding, no failure.
5 Compete & Play 5–8 min
A game or finisher to end on a high — racing, battling, skipping, or free play. This is the part they remember.
6 Cool-Down 3–5 min
Slow the breath and stretch gently to start recovery before heading off.
HOW EACH EXERCISE READS
Every exercise is written the same simple way:
01 EXERCISE — NAME
The movement. The name is a live link — tap it for the full video demo and step-by-step guide on the site.
SETS × REPS / TIME
How many rounds, and reps, seconds, or metres per round. A range means do what looks good that day.
CUE · QUALITY
The one thing to focus on, and how it should feel. “X” means move fast and snappy; quiet, soft, and stuck means a controlled landing.
REST
How long to rest before the next round. Rest is long on purpose — we want fresh, fast, quality reps, not a burn.
04 What You'll Need
THE kit.
Wonderfully simple. A band, a little space, and a few household bits. No gym, no weights, no cost barrier — just room to move and play.
01 Loop resistance band set
Light and medium tension is plenty. The band adds gentle, safe resistance to squats, lunges, and throws — no heavy weights needed.
02 A low box or sturdy step
Shin-to-knee height. For step-ups, step-downs, and low box hop-ups with soft landings.
03 A bit of open space
A driveway, yard, hallway, or garage — about 10 metres to skip, bound, and sprint. A few cones or markers help for the games.
04 A skipping rope & light ball
A jump rope for footwork and fun, and a light ball for the rotational “hockey-shot” throws. A wall to throw against is handy.
That is it. Everything in this program can be done at home, in a garage, or at the park — with a band, a step, and space to play.

A quick check for parents & coaches
SAFETY & FUN FIRST
This is a development program for young athletes — fun and good movement come before anything else. Keep these in mind every session.
It should feel like play. If a child dreads it, lighten it and keep it fun.
Good shoes, a flat non-slip surface, and a clear space to move.
Every rep is about quality, not tiredness — never train “to failure.”
Stop any movement that causes sharp pain, and rest when tired or unwell.
An adult supervises, and the warm-up is done before the fast or jumping work.
Kids grow and develop at different rates — that is completely normal. Scale every movement to the child in front of you (see the Make It Easier / Harder library).
06 The Skills Check
SEE your progress.
Five simple, measurable tests — every one gives you a number to beat. No gym needed. Do the same five, in the same order, three times: Week 1, Week 4, and Week 8.
Always warm up with the RAMP first, use the same shoes, surface, and timing method, and keep it positive. Write down your best number — then beat it later.
01 +5–10 cm
Broad Jump ↗
Power · jump distance
Best of 3 · measure in cm
Stand still, swing the arms, and jump as far as you can off two feet. Stick the landing. Measure to the back heel.
8-Week Aim ▲ +5–10 cm
02 −0.1 to −0.3 s
20m Sprint ↗
Acceleration & speed
Best of 2 · full rest · time in s
From a standing start, sprint a marked 20m as fast as you can. Time it with a phone timer or a partner.
8-Week Aim ▲ −0.1 to −0.3 s
03 −0.1 to −0.3 s
20m Flying Sprint ↗
Top speed
Best of 2 · time in s
Take a 10–15m run-up to build speed, then time only the 20m at full speed. This tests top speed, not the start.
8-Week Aim ▲ −0.1 to −0.3 s
04 +15–30 s
Iso Wall Sit ↗
Leg strength · max hold
1 max hold · time in s
Sit against a wall, knees at 90°, thighs flat. Hold as long as you can with good form. Stop when the hips drop.
8-Week Aim ▲ +15–30 s
05 +10–20 s
SL Balance — Eyes Closed ↗
Balance · max hold
Best per leg · time in s
Balance on one foot with your eyes closed. Count the seconds until you wobble or touch down. Test both legs.
8-Week Aim ▲ +10–20 s

BLOCK 1 · WEEKS 1–4
MOVE & LAND
“Move well first. Land like a cat.”
Block 1 is all about the basics that make a great athlete: standing strong, squatting and hinging well, balancing on one leg, skipping with good rhythm, and — most important at this age — landing softly and under control. Every jump is a stick-and-freeze, not a bounce, so the body learns to absorb force safely.
The work climbs gently for three weeks, then Week 4 is an easy week with a quick skills check. Nothing is to failure. Quality, fun, and confidence are the whole point.

BLOCK 2 · WEEKS 5–8
SPRING & PLAY
“Now turn good movement into speed.”
With the basics owned, Block 2 adds spring: lateral bounds, broad jumps into sprints, light medicine-ball throws for a hockey-shot feel, and reactive change-of-direction games. Landings are still soft, volume is still low, and rest is still long — we are building springs, not grinding.
The work peaks in Week 7 with everything at game speed, then Week 8 lightens up so you feel fast and fresh for a final, fun skills check. Eight weeks, one confident young athlete.

09 Recovery & Growth
WHERE KIDS get better.
Young athletes improve between sessions — when they sleep, eat, rest, and play. The training is the spark; recovery is where it actually sticks.
Sleep
9–11 hours a night for this age. Sleep is when the body grows and the brain locks in new skills. A good night’s sleep does more for a young athlete than any single drill.
Fuel
Regular meals with plenty of variety — protein, fruit and vegetables, and carbs for energy. Eat breakfast before a session, and have a snack and water afterward.
Play is training
Free play, other sports, and time at the park all build coordination and joy of movement. Encourage lots of it — variety makes better, healthier athletes than early specialization.
Rest days & listening in
Leave a day between sessions, and take extra rest when a child is tired, sore, or just not into it. A little muscle soreness is normal; sharp pain is a stop sign — and growing bodies need patience.
10 How To Train This
FIVE simple rules.
For the player, the parent, and the coach. Follow these and the program takes care of itself.
01 Quality, not tiredness.
Every rep should look good and feel snappy. A few crisp reps with rest beat lots of sloppy, tired ones. If the movement gets messy, the set is done.
02 Have fun.
This is play with a purpose. Make games out of the drills, keep the energy up, and finish wanting more. A kid who enjoys it will keep coming back — that is the real win.
03 Stick every landing.
Soft, quiet, balanced landings are the most important skill at this age. “Land like a cat” protects young joints and builds the springs for later. Freeze and hold it.
04 Take the easy weeks.
Weeks 4 and 8 are lighter on purpose. Don’t add more because it feels easy — feeling fresh is exactly what lets the skills show up on the check.
05 Track your wins.
Fill in the skill-check pages. Seeing a jump get longer or a balance get steadier is huge motivation — proof the work is paying off.
