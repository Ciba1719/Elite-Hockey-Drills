01 — The Method

Train to compete.

Between the ages of 15 and 18, you're in what sport scientists call the "Train to Compete" phase of the Long-Term Athletic Development (LTAD) model. Your nervous system is wired to absorb power and speed work like a sponge, your tendons and bones are responding to load, and your body is laying down the structural strength you'll lean on for the next decade. This is the window where the trainable becomes trained. We don't waste it — five days, bodyweight-led with creative loading, real intensity, quality every rep.

01

Power Is the Priority

Plyometrics, jumps, and short sprints sit at the center of every session. Rate of Force Development — how quickly your muscles produce force — is the most trainable quality you have right now.

02

Single-Leg First

Skating is a single-leg sport. Every stride is one leg at a time. Split squats, single-leg RDLs, and lateral bounds transfer more to the ice than any barbell back squat ever will.

03

Technique Is Non-Negotiable

Habits cement at your age. Ugly reps now become injuries later. We move well before we move fast. If a rep looks ugly, slow down — every time.

"The teen years are when the trainable becomes trained. Train smart now — your 22-year-old self is watching."
02 — How To Use This

Five days. One week.

Pick five training days that work for you — they don't need to be Monday through Friday. Leave at least one full rest day mid-week. Each session is 35 to 45 minutes including the warm-up and cool-down. You need a small open space and a floor. A backpack with a few books, or a water jug, covers any loading you'll need. If something hurts — actually hurts, not "burns" — stop. Pain is information. Listen to it.

How To Read a Workout

Every exercise card has the same parts. Tap any exercise name — in blue — to open a demo video and the full step-by-step guide on the site.

Sets × Reps
The volume — how many rounds, and how many reps, seconds, or metres per round.
Tempo
The speed of each rep. A 3-1-1-0 tempo means 3 seconds lowering, 1 second pause at the bottom, 1 second up, 0 rest at the top.
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion, 1–10 — how hard the set feels. The simplest, most honest way to regulate intensity. We mostly live in the 6–8 range; save 9+ for true test sets.
Rest · Cue · Builds
How long to wait before the next set, the one thing to focus on, and the hockey reason for the work.

Before Every Session

The warm-up.

Three movements. Five minutes. Every single day before you train. The warm-up isn't optional — it's how you raise body temperature, prime your nervous system, and make sure the first hard rep of the session isn't your first proper rep of the day. Move with control. Breathe through your nose. Don't rush.

· Warm-Up~5 min
01World's Greatest Stretch↗
2 rounds3 each side

Start in a push-up position. Step your right foot to the outside of your right hand. Drop your right elbow toward the floor for a beat. Then rotate your right arm up to the ceiling, eyes following. Replant. Step back. Switch sides.

"Open the hip. Open the chest. One movement, three jobs."

Builds — Hip mobility, T-spine rotation, and a long, ready spine — three things every skater needs before loaded work.

02Inchworm + Shoulder Tap↗
2 rounds5 reps

Stand tall. Bend forward and walk your hands out into a long plank. Once stable, tap your right hand to your left shoulder, then left to right — keep your hips square and don't let them rock. Walk your hands back to your feet. Stand. Repeat.

"Hips locked. Don't rock — squeeze the floor away."

Builds — Full-body activation, anti-rotation core engagement, and shoulder stability before you load the upper body.

03A-Skip for Distance↗
2 rounds20m

Skip forward. Drive the front knee to hip height. Pump the opposite arm hard — hand from hip to cheek. Land tall on the ball of your foot. Stay vertical. This is not jogging — it's a tall, springy, mechanically clean drill.

"Tall and bouncy. Knee up, arm through — like you've got springs."

Builds — Sprint posture, ankle stiffness, and the arm-leg coordination that turns into top-end speed.

Day 1
Build the Engine

Lower Power + Strength

~40 minBackpack + bodyweight

Today is the foundation. Heavy single-leg patterns paired with bodyweight power work. We loaded the backpack for a reason — load drives adaptation. Quality is the rule. If a rep gets ugly, drop the weight or skip a rep. Rest fully between sets. You're not trying to be tired — you're trying to be strong. Begin with the warm-up.

· The Workout6 movements
01Goblet Squat (Backpack)↗
4 × 6Tempo 3-1-1-0RPE 7REST 90s

Backpack hugged tight to your chest, elbows pointing down. Feet a little wider than shoulders, toes slightly out. Push your hips back, knees track over your toes, descend until your thighs are at least parallel. Chest stays tall. Drive through the whole foot to stand. The slow 3-second descent is the point — that's where strength is built.

"Slow down on the way down. Stand up like you mean it."

Builds — Bilateral leg strength and the trunk control that lets your hips drive force into the ice on every stride.

02Bulgarian Split Squat↗
3 × 6each leg · Tempo 2-1-1-0RPE 7-8REST 60s

Rear foot up on a chair, couch, or bench (about knee height). Front foot a long stride forward. Drop your back knee straight toward the floor — don't crash. Front knee tracks over the front foot. Drive through the front heel to stand. The front leg does the work; the back leg is just for balance.

"Front foot loaded. Tall chest. Back knee whispers to the floor."

Builds — Single-leg strength under load — the exact pattern of a hard skating push. Most teen players are weaker on one side. You'll find yours today.

03Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift↗
3 × 6each leg · slow tempoRPE 6-7REST 60s

Stand on your right leg, a soft micro-bend you hold the whole rep. Hinge from your hip — push your butt straight back as your free leg lifts behind you. Body stays one long line from heel to head. Lower as far as your hamstring allows without your back rounding. Stand back up by squeezing the glute of the standing leg.

"Long spine. Hip back, not knee down."

Builds — Posterior chain strength — hamstrings, glutes, lower back — plus the single-leg balance that protects you from groin and adductor issues.

04Broad Jump — Stick It↗
4 × 3full effortREST 90s

Feet shoulder-width. Big arm swing back, deep counter-movement, jump forward as far as you can. The non-negotiable: stick every landing. Bent knees, hips back, perfectly balanced — hold three full seconds before resetting. If you can't stick it, you jumped too far.

"Jump far. Land like a statue. Soft feet, stiff core."

Builds — Concentric power (the jump) and eccentric absorption (the landing) — both required for explosive, injury-resistant skating starts.

05Side Plank + Top-Leg Lift↗
3 × 8each side · controlledREST 45s

Side plank on your forearm — elbow under shoulder, body in a straight line from ankles to head. Hold the plank position perfectly still while you slowly lift and lower the top leg. Hips stay stacked — do not let them rotate or sag. The lift is small. The challenge is staying square.

"Hips stacked. Don't twist — the floor wants you to twist."

Builds — Anti-rotation and lateral hip strength — the foundation of stable skating and the spine-saving counter to all the rotation hockey demands.

06Glute Bridge March↗
3 × 8each leg · controlledREST 45s

On your back, knees bent, feet a hand-width from your butt. Drive your hips up into a full glute bridge — straight line knees to shoulders. From there, slowly march one knee up toward your chest while keeping your hips perfectly level. Lower that foot. Switch. Don't let your hips drop.

"Hips up. Hips level. Glutes do the work."

Builds — Glute endurance and pelvic control — the engine of every stride and the off-switch for the lower-back pain that haunts teen hockey players.

Cool-Down — 2 min

Lie on your back, knees bent. Breathe in for 4 seconds, out for 6 seconds. Ten breaths. Your nervous system shifts from "go" to "recover" — that shift is when adaptation begins.

Day 2
Get Fast, Stay Fresh

Speed Day

~35 minOpen space + 2 markers

Speed is the most trainable quality you have right now — and the most ruined by fatigue. Today you rest fully between every effort. If you're gassed, you're not training speed anymore — you're training your body to be slow under fatigue, which is the opposite of the point. Every rep is fresh. Every rep is your fastest. Begin with the warm-up.

· The Workout5 movements
01Wall Drive — Drive Hold↗
4 × 8seach legREST 30s

Arm's length from a wall. Hands flat, lean at roughly 45° — body in a perfect straight line head to heels. Drive one knee up to hip height and hold for 8 seconds. The standing leg pushes the floor away the entire hold. Switch legs. Posture is everything: no broken hips, no sagging core.

"Push the floor away. Knee up like punching the sky."

Builds — Sprint posture and the exact angle of acceleration from a stop — the mechanics of your first three steps off the blue line.

02A-Skip for Distance↗
3 × 20mwalk-back rest

Skip forward, but make it athletic. Drive the front knee high — hip height, every rep. Hand from hip to cheek on the opposite arm, hard pump. Land tall on the ball of the foot. Stay vertical the whole time. This is technique work — speed comes later.

"Tall. Bouncy. Mechanically perfect — speed is built here."

Builds — Sprint mechanics — the foot strike, knee drive, and arm action that translate directly to top-end skating speed.

03Falling-Start Sprint↗
6 × 10mfull restREST 90s

Stand tall, on the balls of your feet, body as a single straight line. Lean forward from the ankles — keep falling until the only way to stop yourself is to sprint. That first step happens when gravity has already started the work. Sprint hard for 10 meters. Walk back. Reset.

"Fall first. Step second. Don't think about going — go."

Builds — First-step explosiveness — the ability to win the race to a loose puck and beat a defender to a gap.

04Lateral Bound — Stick It↗
4 × 4each sideREST 60s

Stand on your right leg, knee soft. Push off sideways to your left, exploding through the right foot. Land on your left leg only — stick it. Bent knee, balanced, no wobble. Hold 2 seconds. Push back to the right. That's two reps. Distance matters less than the quality of the landing.

"Push hard. Land harder. Freeze before you fly again."

Builds — The exact mechanics of a skating crossover and a hard edge change. Lateral force production is what separates fast skaters from quick skaters.

05Shuttle — 5m / 10m / 5m↗
4 roundsfull restREST 90s

Two markers 5 meters apart, plus a third 10 meters past the first. Start at marker A. Sprint to B (5m), plant and turn, sprint back through A to C (10m), plant and turn, sprint back to A. Every change of direction matters — get low, plant the outside foot, drive the new way.

"Low on the turn. Drive out, don't drift out."

Builds — Acceleration, deceleration, and change of direction — the three speeds that decide most plays in a hockey shift.

Cool-Down — 2 min

Easy walk 90 seconds. Then 10 slow ankle circles each direction, each foot. Sprint days hammer the calves and feet — pay them back.

Day 3
Protect the Middle

Upper + Anti-Rot Core

~40 minBackpack + sturdy table/bar

Hockey is a rotational sport. Shots, checks, battles — your trunk is constantly being twisted by outside forces. The fix isn't more crunches. It's training the core to resist rotation while the rest of you produces it. Pair that with smart upper-body pushing and pulling and you become hard to move off the puck. Begin with the warm-up.

Tap any exercise to watch the demo and open the full guide
· The Workout6 movements
01Push-Up — Tempo↗
4 × 8-10Tempo 2-1-1-0RPE 7REST 60s

Hands directly under shoulders, body in one straight line head to heels. Brace your core like someone is about to hit it. Lower over 2 seconds, pause 1 second a fist's-height from the floor, drive up. If you can't keep the line, drop to your knees — quality first.

"Straight line. Squeeze the floor away. Don't sag."

Builds — Upper-body push strength and the rigid trunk you need for board battles and stick-to-stick contact.

02Inverted Row (under a sturdy table or bar)↗
3 × 8Tempo 2-0-1-1RPE 7REST 75s

Lie under a sturdy table or low bar. Grip with hands just outside shoulders. Body in a straight line, heels on the floor (or feet elevated for harder). Pull your chest up to meet the bar/table edge, pause 1 second at the top, lower over 2 seconds. Shoulders pull down and back — don't shrug.

"Pull with the back, not the arms. Squeeze the shoulder blades."

Builds — Pulling strength and posture — the counterbalance to all the pushing hockey players do, and a critical guard against rounded-shoulder posture.

03Pike Push-Up↗
3 × 6Tempo 2-1-1-0RPE 7REST 75s

Start in a downward-dog — hips high, body an upside-down V, hands a little wider than shoulders. Bend your elbows and lower the top of your head between your hands. Stop just before contact. Press back to the start. If too hard, feet stay low (less vertical angle) — make it work first.

"Hips high. Press the floor away from your head."

Builds — Vertical pushing strength and shoulder stability — the foundation for harder overhead work as you progress.

04Renegade Row (Backpack)↗
3 × 6each side · controlledREST 60s

High plank, one hand on a backpack handle (or grip the strap), one hand on the floor. Body in a straight line. Without letting your hips rotate at all, row the backpack up to your ribs. Lower with control. Switch sides. The whole point is that your hips don't move — the bag does.

"Hips locked. The bag moves — you don't."

Builds — Anti-rotation core under load — the exact quality you need when an opponent is trying to twist you off the puck.

05Half-Kneeling Anti-Rotation Hold↗
3 × 20seach sideREST 45s

Half-kneeling — one knee down, one foot forward, both knees bent 90°. Press a backpack out in front of your chest with both hands (or hold a band pulling laterally). A partner pulls sideways on the bag, or the band pulls you. Resist. Don't rotate. Don't lean. Hold the position.

"Stay square. Whatever pulls you — you out-pull it."

Builds — The deep core's ability to keep the spine stable while the limbs work — the trait that lets a great defender stay strong on his stick.

06Hollow Body Hold↗
3 × 30sbreathe steadyREST 45s

On your back. Press your lower back firmly into the floor — non-negotiable. Lift shoulder blades off the ground, arms by your ears, legs straight and 6 inches off the floor. Body looks like a shallow banana. Breathe shallow but steady. If your back lifts, raise your arms or legs higher — easier.

"Glue your back to the floor. Glue it."

Builds — Anterior core strength and the rigid trunk that transfers force from your hips to your hands — every shot, every stick check.

Cool-Down — 2 min

Child's pose, 60 seconds, deep breathing. Then thread-the-needle stretch, 30 seconds each side. Open up the T-spine after all that pushing and pulling.

Day 4
Train the Shift

Conditioning + Mobility

~35 minOpen space

Hockey isn't a marathon and it isn't a sprint. It's 45 seconds of war, followed by 2 minutes on the bench, repeated for 60 minutes. Today we train that exact pattern — work to rest, work to rest. After the conditioning, we put the joints back together with a focused mobility flow. Skip the mobility and tomorrow you'll feel like a brick. Begin with the warm-up.

1 The Shift Simulator~13 min

5 rounds of a shift, repeated. Each 45-second work block is three 15-second efforts back to back — no rest between them — then walk and breathe for 90 seconds. The 1:2 work-to-rest ratio is exactly what your shifts look like in a real game.

2 The Mobility Flow~6 min

Four movements to open the body back up. Slow and controlled.

0190/90 Hip Switches↗
2 × 8slow, controlled

Sit with both knees bent 90°, one leg in front, one to the side. Rotate the hips to switch sides without using your hands. Opens the hips in both internal and external rotation.

02T-Spine Open Book↗
2 × 8each side

Lie on your side, knees bent, both arms straight out in front. Slowly rotate the top arm in a wide arc to the floor behind you — knees stay together. Open the chest. Breathe into the stretch.

03Couch Stretch↗
2 × 45seach side

Kneel, back foot up against a wall (top of foot flat on the wall, shin vertical). Front foot forward. Squeeze the glute of the back leg hard and stand tall through the back hip. You'll feel the front of the back-leg hip.

04Active Hamstring Floss↗
2 × 6each side

On your back. Hug one knee to your chest. Slowly straighten that leg toward the ceiling, then bend it back. Move through full range — don't bounce, don't yank. The hamstring lengthens because you're asking, not forcing.

Bonus

The coaching voice.

Every great athlete has a coach in their head. These are the lines that should be running through yours when there's no one in the gym with you. Memorize them. Repeat them. They'll save you from the bad reps that compound into bad habits.

Cue 01 · On Technique
"If a rep looks ugly, slow down."

Ugly reps cement bad patterns into your nervous system. A clean rep at slower speed beats a fast, broken rep every single time. Quality is the whole game.

Cue 02 · On This Window
"Train the trainable now — it isn't coming back."

Speed and power are most adaptable in your teen years. The work you do now sets a ceiling you'll either reach in your twenties — or never reach. There is no later.

Cue 03 · On Landing
"Be a great lander before you're a great jumper."

The athletes who stay healthy aren't the ones who jump highest — they're the ones who absorb force best. Every plyometric rep is two skills: the explosion, and the catch. The catch matters more.

Cue 04 · On Competition
"Compete with yesterday's version of you."

Forget your teammate's numbers. Forget what's on Instagram. The only honest competition is whether today's effort is better than last week's. Track it. Beat it.

Cue 05 · On Recovery
"You don't get better in the gym. You get better between gyms."

Training is the stimulus. Sleep, food, and rest are where adaptation actually happens. Ignore the recovery half and you do half the work for half the results.

Cue 06 · On Show-Up Days
"Some days you train hard. Some days you train smart. Never skip."

Every athlete has off days — flat legs, no spark. On those days you don't push max effort, you push perfect technique at low load. The streak matters more than any single session.

Why some days you feel flat

Some training days you feel sharp — fast, light, ready. Other days the same exercises feel like wading through mud. That's not weakness. That's your central nervous system (CNS) running on a different schedule than your muscles. Heavy sleep deficit, exam stress, a hard practice the day before — all of it taxes the CNS. Muscles recover in hours; the CNS takes longer. The cue: on a flat day, lower the intensity, raise the focus. Train the technique, not the maximum. Then go sleep.

The Other Half

Recovery is when you get better.

Training is the stimulus. Recovery is when your body actually adapts to it. Muscles grow, tendons stiffen, the nervous system reorganizes — all of it happens while you're not training. Get this part wrong and the five days of work do half of what they could. Get it right and you'll feel the difference inside two weeks.

8–10 hrs

Sleep

Growth hormone — the chemical that builds muscle and bone — releases in deep sleep. Teens between 15 and 18 actually need more sleep than younger kids, not less. Phones off 60 minutes before bed. Same sleep window every night if you can. This is the single most powerful performance lever you control.

3 parts

Fuel

A protein (chicken, fish, eggs, beef, lentils), a carb (rice, potato, pasta, fruit, bread), a vegetable. That's the rule. Eat within 60 minutes after every training session — protein + carb. Don't skip breakfast. We don't count calories at your age. We eat real food often.

8–10 glasses

Hydration

Performance drops measurably at just 2% dehydration — that's how much fluid you lose in one hard session. Drink throughout the day, not just at training. The honest test: if your pee is darker than pale lemonade, you're already behind. Keep a bottle with you everywhere.

"You can't out-train bad sleep, bad food, and bad hydration. Not at any age. Especially not at yours."
