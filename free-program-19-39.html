01 — The Method

Train hard. Train smart.

You are in your physical prime — what sport scientists call the "Train to Win" window of long-term athletic development. Strength, power, and aerobic capacity aren't just trainable in your twenties and thirties — they're expected. What you put in off the ice now determines what you carry onto the ice every shift. Most adult players train like fitness enthusiasts, not athletes — and generic gym programs don't transfer. Your sport is specific. Your training should be too. These five days are bodyweight-only, built for players training around work, family, travel, and limited time.

01

Specificity

The SAID principle — your body adapts specifically to what you ask of it. Random training produces random results. Every exercise here is chosen for one reason: your next shift.

02

Intensity

Bodyweight doesn't mean easy. Tempo, leverage, and unilateral work scale intensity to any level. If a rep is too easy, make it harder before you make it longer.

03

Recovery = Adaptation

Training breaks you down. Sleep, food, and managed stress build you back up. Skip recovery and you've trained for nothing.

"Quality over speed. If a rep looks ugly, slow down."
02 — How To Use This

Five days. One week.

Pick five days that fit your schedule — they don't need to be Monday through Friday. The recommended rhythm is two-on, one-off, two-on, one-off. Each session runs 30–45 minutes including warm-up and cool-down. You need a small open space and a floor. No equipment. If something hurts (not "burns" — actually hurts), stop. The goal is to walk into next week stronger, not into a clinic.

How To Read a Workout

Every exercise card has the same parts. Tap any exercise name — in blue — to open a demo video and the full step-by-step guide on the site.

Sets × Reps
The volume — how many sets, and how many reps, seconds, or metres each.
Tempo
The speed of each rep. A 3-1-1-0 tempo means 3 seconds lowering, 1 second pause, 1 second up, 0 rest at the top.
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion, 1–10. RPE 8 means 2 reps left in the tank, RPE 9 means 1 left, RPE 10 is failure. Most quality work lives at RPE 7–8 — higher isn't always better.
Rest · Cue · Builds
How long to wait before the next set, the one thing to focus on, and the hockey reason for the work.

Before Every Session

The warm-up.

Three movements. Five minutes. Every session. The warm-up is not optional — it's how you bring your nervous system online, open the joints hockey demands, and avoid the silly injuries that derail a training week. Move with control. Breathe through your nose if you can.

· Warm-Up~5 min
0190/90 Hip Switch↗
2 rounds6 each side

Sit on the floor with one leg in front at 90° (shin parallel to your body) and the other leg behind at 90°. Keep both knees on the floor. Rotate the hips to switch sides. Hands light on the floor for balance.

"Drive the back knee through the floor."

Builds — Hip internal and external rotation — the range you need for a clean stride and a powerful crossover.

02World's Greatest Stretch↗
2 rounds4 each side

From a push-up position, step your right foot outside your right hand. Drop your right elbow toward the floor. Reach your right hand to the ceiling, opening the chest. Place the hand back down, step back, switch.

"Rotate from the mid-back, not the lower back."

Builds — T-spine rotation, hip mobility, and full posterior chain prep in one movement.

03A-Skip↗
2 rounds20m

Skip forward with exaggerated knee drive. Front knee comes up to hip height. Opposite arm pumps hard. Land softly on the ball of the foot. Tall posture throughout — this is not jogging.

"Tall and bouncy. Drive the knee — pump the arm."

Builds — CNS activation and sprint-pattern priming. Wakes the nervous system before fast work.

Day 1
The Foundation

Max Strength

~40 minBodyweight only

Today builds the engine of your skating. Strength at this stage is about controlled, heavy-feeling work with bodyweight — using tempo, leverage, and unilateral positions to create real intensity. We're not chasing exhaustion. We're chasing quality reps that demand everything from your legs. Begin with the warm-up — always.

Tap any exercise to watch the demo and open the full guide
· The Workout4 movements
01Bulgarian Split Squat↗
4 × 8each leg · Tempo 4-1-1-0RPE 8REST 90s

Stand a long stride from a low surface (couch, bench, chair). Place the laces of your back foot on the surface, weight on the front leg. Drop the back knee straight down toward the floor. Front knee tracks over your second toe. Drive through the heel and mid-foot. The front leg does 90% of the work.

"Front knee tracks the toe. Back leg is just a kickstand."

Builds — Unilateral lower-body strength — the exact pattern of a skating stride, where one leg drives while the other prepares to fire.

02Pistol Squat Progression↗
3 × 5each leg · Tempo 3-2-1-0RPE 8REST 90s

Stand on one leg with the other extended forward, heel hovering. Sit back and down as far as you control, hips behind the heel. Use a doorframe or TRX for hand support if needed — this is a progression, not an ego lift. Drive through the full foot to stand.

"Sit between your heels — not over them."

Builds — Maximal single-leg strength and ankle/hip mobility under load. The most hockey-specific bodyweight strength movement there is.

03Cossack Squat↗
3 × 6each side · Tempo 3-1-1-0RPE 7REST 60s

Wide stance, toes turned slightly out. Shift your weight to one side, bending that knee deeply while the other leg stays straight, heel down. Chest tall. The straight-leg toes point up. Return through center, then shift to the other side.

"Long leg, heel down. Deep leg, heel down. No cheating."

Builds — Lateral strength and adductor control — the muscles that win edge battles and absorb hits on the boards.

04Nordic Hamstring Curl↗
3 × 5Tempo 5-0-X-0RPE 9REST 90s

Kneel on a soft surface. Anchor your ankles (partner, loaded couch, sturdy bar). Arms crossed at chest. Hinge forward from the knees only — body in a straight line knee-to-head — lowering as slowly as you can. Catch yourself with your hands at the bottom and push back up. The eccentric is the whole point.

Day 2
Get Fast

Speed & Power

~35 minOpen space needed

Today is short, sharp, and fully recovered. Plyometrics and sprint work are CNS-driven — they need a fresh nervous system, not a tired body. You'll do less total volume than yesterday and rest longer between sets. If you rush rest periods, you train conditioning, not speed. Discipline yourself. Begin with the warm-up — don't skip.

Tap any exercise to watch the demo and open the full guide
· The Workout4 movements
01Broad Jump↗
5 × 3full rest (~90s)RPE 9

Feet shoulder-width, arms swing back, jump forward as far as you can. Land in a quarter-squat, both feet together, hold for 2 seconds. Walk back to the start. No rebound reps — every jump starts from a dead stop.

"Maximum jump. Frozen landing."

Builds — Lower-body concentric power and the eccentric landing strength that protects your knees and hips through hockey's deceleration demands.

02Lateral Bound↗
4 × 4each sideRPE 8REST 60s

Stand on the right leg. Push laterally off the right and land on the left in a soft athletic position. Pause one full second to confirm balance. Push back to the right. Two reps total.

"Stick the landing for one full second. Then go."

Builds — Lateral power production and single-leg deceleration — the exact pattern of every push and stop on the ice.

03Depth Drop to Stick↗
3 × 5freeze on impactRPE 7REST 60s

Stand on a low surface (step, thick book, low couch — knee-height max). Step off (don't jump) and land in a quarter-squat, freezing on impact. Hold 3 seconds. The drop teaches your tendons and connective tissue to absorb force — the foundation for higher-level reactive work.

"Land soft. Stop dead. Eyes forward."

Builds — Reactive strength and tendon stiffness — the elastic quality that converts ground contact into propulsion.

0430-Meter Acceleration Sprint↗
6 repsfull rest (~90s)RPE 9-10

Start from a 2-point staggered stance, body angled forward. Drive out hard, accelerating over 30 meters. The first 10 meters are pure piston action — knee drive, low body angle. Walk back. Reset. Repeat.

"First three steps are the workout. Make them perfect."

Builds — First-step acceleration — the most hockey-specific speed quality. Most loose pucks are won in the first three strides.

Day 3
Drive the Chain

Upper & Posterior

~40 minBodyweight only

Hockey is a posterior-chain sport. Your back, glutes, and hamstrings drive every stride, every shot, every battle. Today builds those — alongside upper-body pushing and pulling strength that holds up under physical play. Anti-rotation core finishes the session, because spine stability is what keeps the rest of this work translating to the ice. Begin with the warm-up.

Tap any exercise to watch the demo and open the full guide
· The Workout4 movements
01Archer Push-Up↗
4 × 5each side · Tempo 3-1-1-0RPE 8REST 75s

Wide push-up position, arms about 1.5× shoulder width apart. Lower toward one hand while the other arm stays straight (it's a brace). The working side does most of the work. Drive back up. Alternate sides. Knees down is a fine regression.

"Sink to one side. Brace with the other."

Builds — Unilateral pushing strength — closer to a one-arm push-up than a regular one. Builds the upper body that holds its ground in puck battles.

02Single-Leg RDL↗
4 × 8each leg · Tempo 3-1-1-0RPE 8REST 75s

Stand on one leg, slight bend in the knee. Hinge at the hip — chest goes forward, free leg goes back, body forms a T parallel to the floor. Reach the opposite hand toward the standing foot. Return to standing under control. Balance is part of the exercise.

"Hips square. Long line — head to back heel."

Builds — Hamstring and glute strength under length — the exact loading pattern your stride needs to extend powerfully.

03Pull-Up or Inverted Row↗
4 × 6-8Tempo 2-1-2-0RPE 8REST 90s

Pull-up: hands shoulder-width, full hang, pull until chin clears the bar, lower under control over 2 seconds. No bar? Inverted row off a sturdy table edge or under a desk — heels on the floor, body straight, pull chest to the surface.

"Lead with the chest. Squeeze the shoulder blades."

Builds — Vertical and horizontal pulling strength. Counter-balances the pushing demands of the sport and protects shoulder health.

04Single-Leg Elevated Hip Thrust↗
3 × 10each leg · 2s pause at topRPE 8REST 60s

Shoulders on a couch or low bench, knees bent, one foot flat on the floor. The other leg extends straight out. Drive through the planted heel and lift the hips until they form a straight line knees-to-shoulders. Squeeze the glute hard at the top for 2 seconds. Lower slowly.

"Squeeze the glute. Don't arch the lower back."

Builds — Glute power on a single leg — the actual driver of the skating stride. Most adult players have weak, sleepy glutes. Today they wake up.

Day 4
Train the Shift

Condition Like You Play

~35 minOpen space or stairs

A hockey shift is roughly 45 seconds of all-out work followed by 2–4 minutes of rest. That ratio — about 1:3 to 1:5 — is what your conditioning should mirror. Steady-state jogging doesn't train this. Long intervals don't train this. What trains it is repeated short maximal efforts with full recovery. This work sits on Day 4 deliberately, after Day 3's lift, to avoid concurrent-training interference. Begin with the warm-up.

Tap any exercise to watch the demo and open the full guide
· The Workout4 movements
01Shift Shuttle Intervals↗
8 roundswork 30s : rest 60sRPE 9

30 seconds of maximum-effort shuttle running between two markers 15m apart. 60 seconds of complete rest — walk, breathe, drink. The hockey work-to-rest ratio. The first round will feel easy. The last round will not.

"Round 8 looks like round 1. That's the standard."

Builds — Anaerobic capacity at hockey's exact work-to-rest ratio. Trains your body to recover between shifts and come back fresh.

02Burpee-to-Bound Complex↗
5 roundswork 20s : rest 40sRPE 9

20 seconds continuous: burpee → broad jump → drop and burpee → broad jump. 40 seconds of full rest. Five rounds total. The complex puts you horizontal and vertical, fast and powerful — like the unpredictability of a real shift.

"Every burpee is a deliberate position, not a flop."

Builds — Mixed-modal anaerobic power. Trains the heart, lungs, and legs to handle hockey's chaos.

03Bear-Crawl for Distance↗
4 rounds20mRPE 8REST 60s

On hands and feet, knees an inch off the floor. Drive forward 20 meters as fast as you can while holding the position. Walk back. Repeat. This is the closest bodyweight substitute for a heavy sled push.

"Hips low. Drive the floor backward."

Builds — Full-body horizontal power production. Mimics the body angle of a hard acceleration.

04Breath Reset↗
2 minactive recovery

After the last interval, walk slowly. Hands on head — opens the chest. Nasal inhale 4 seconds, mouth exhale 6 seconds. Continuous for 2 minutes. This is the active recovery your nervous system needs to switch out of fight-or-flight.

"Slow your breath. Slow your heart."

Day 5
Tie It Together

Hockey Integration

~45 min4 rounds · 2 min rest between

The last day pulls everything together. Strength, speed, and conditioning in one circuit at game-pace. This is what the program has been building toward — a session that feels like a high-pressure shift. Pace yourself. Form first. The goal isn't to survive the circuit. It's to attack each round at the level you'd attack each shift. Begin with the warm-up — do it twice today.

· The Circuit4 rounds · 2 min rest

Move station to station with minimal rest within a round; 2 minutes rest between rounds. Each round is one "shift" — finish the work in roughly 60–75 seconds. That's the upper end of a long shift.

A1Split-Squat Jump↗
6 each legRPE 8

Bulgarian split-squat position. Jump up explosively, switch legs in the air, land in the opposite split. The eccentric landing matters as much as the jump.

"Hip drives the jump. Soft on the landing."

A2Lateral Bound↗
4 each sideRPE 7

From single-leg, bound sideways and stick the landing for one full second before pushing back.

"Stick it. Then go."

A3Push-Up to Shoulder Tap↗
8 each sideRPE 7

Push-up plank. After each push-up, tap the opposite shoulder with one hand without letting the hips rotate.

"Stay square. Hips don't move."

A4Sprint↗
20mRPE 9

Maximum effort 20-meter sprint between two markers. Walk back. The sprint is your shift.

"This is your shift. Make it count."

Bonus

Shift-ready conditioning.

Three protocols, fifteen minutes. Drop-in conditioning sessions for a rest day, a travel day, or any time you have 15 minutes and a heartbeat. All built on the same hockey work-to-rest principle. Pick one. Time it. Beat it next time.

01

Tabata Protocol

Need: timer · 4 min total

20 seconds work, 10 seconds rest, 8 rounds. Pick one movement — burpees, sprint-in-place, mountain climbers, jump squats — and go all-out for each 20-second window. Don't pace it. Don't be smart. Maximum effort, every window.

Anaerobic power, lactate tolerance, mental edge.
02

Shift Replicator

Need: open space · 14 min total

5 rounds, each one a shift. Each round: 45 seconds maximum-effort shuttle running, then 2 minutes complete rest (walk, breathe). Five "shifts" in 14 minutes. The closest off-ice replica of an actual period of play.

Game-specific conditioning, recovery between shifts.
03

Stair Interval

Need: a flight of stairs · 15 min total

10 rounds: sprint up one flight, walk down. Aim for 8–12 seconds up. The walk down is your full rest. Stop when your sprint-up time slows by more than 2 seconds — that's power dropping, and you're now training the wrong system.

Lower-body power-endurance, perfect for travel days.
04

Zone 2 Walk

Need: nothing · 45–60 min

A brisk, conversational-pace walk — fast enough that you can speak in sentences but not paragraphs. Zone 2 trains mitochondrial function and the aerobic base that powers recovery between high-intensity bouts. The unglamorous work that makes the glamorous work possible.

Aerobic base, recovery, mitochondrial density.
"Every protocol here is short, intense, and recovery-respecting. Long, moderate cardio builds an aerobic base — but it isn't what makes you better at hockey. Use Zone 2 for the base. Use the others for the sport."
The Other Half

Recovery is when you get better.

Training is the stimulus. Recovery is the adaptation. Skip this page and you've trained for nothing. Four levers move the needle more than any single workout you do — and the truth is most adult athletes get these wrong.

7–9 hrs

Sleep

The most underrated performance enhancer available. Deep sleep is when growth hormone releases, when the central nervous system clears training fatigue, when motor patterns consolidate. One short night is recoverable. A pattern of short sleep is not. Phones out of the bedroom. Same window every night. Cooler room beats warm.

1.6–2.2 g protein / kg / day

Fuel

Build every meal around protein first: meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes. Then add a quality carb (rice, potato, oats, fruit) and a vegetable. Eat within an hour after training when you can. We don't count calories. We eat real food, often, with protein at the center.

3–4 L water

Hydration + Alcohol

Hydrate consistently across the day — not chugged at training. A liter in the morning is more valuable than a liter at the rink. Alcohol is a hard truth for adult players: it disrupts sleep architecture, depresses protein synthesis for up to 24 hours, and elevates resting heart rate. You don't have to be a monk. Just understand the cost.

Stress = training load

Manage Stress

Your nervous system doesn't separate work stress from training stress. A hard week at the office is fatigue your body has to recover from too. On those weeks, pull back training volume, prioritize sleep, and accept that adaptation comes from total load — not from the gym alone.

"You can't out-train poor recovery. You either compound it — or compromise it."
