Jump & Plyometric

Vertical Jump (CMJ)

Glutesquadshamstringscalves Alactic / ATP-PC
i.

How to Do It

Stand tall, then in one smooth, fast motion dip into a quarter-squat (the counter-movement) and immediately explode straight up, swinging the arms up for height. Reach as high as you can at the top, then land softly on both feet with the knees bent. To use it as a test: mark your standing reach against a wall, then your jump-and-touch reach — the difference is your jump height. Take 3 attempts with full recovery and keep the best. Pick one arm style — hands on hips or a full arm swing — and use the same one every time you re-test.

ii.

Why It Works

The counter-movement jump is the cleanest field measure of lower-body power and rate of force development. The quick dip pre-stretches the muscles — the stretch-shortening cycle — and how high you rise reflects how much force you can produce, fast. That single quality underpins almost everything explosive an athlete does.

iii.

Hockey Transfer

Vertical power is the engine behind your first explosive strides, hard stops, and the drive to win body position. A higher CMJ almost always shows up on the ice as a quicker, more powerful first step — which is why it's the headline number in nearly every athletic combine.

iv.

Coaching Cues

  • "Dip fast, then explode — no pause at the bottom"
  • "drive the arms up for height"
  • "land soft and balanced, knees bent"
v.

Common Mistakes

Dipping too deep or pausing at the bottom, which kills the stretch-reflex; using a different arm swing between attempts; reaching with the wrong hand or rounding the back at takeoff; not measuring against the same fixed point every time, so the score isn't comparable.

vi.

Progression / Regression

Progression

add a light load (a DB or weight vest) or test single-leg for left-right differences

Regression

hands-on-hips only, or a sub-maximal practice jump to groove the pattern first
